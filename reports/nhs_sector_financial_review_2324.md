# NHS Provider Sector Financial Review
## Financial Year 2023/24 — Annual Outturn Analysis

**Prepared by:** Portfolio Analytics
**Data source:** NHS England Trust Accounts Consolidation (TAC) — 206 providers
**Reporting convention:** NHS Financial Reporting Manual (FReM); IFRS as adopted for the public sector
**Currency:** All monetary values in £m unless stated

---

## Executive Summary

The NHS provider sector recorded a **collective operating deficit of £1.6bn** in 2023/24, representing the worst collective financial position across the three years analysed. This follows a surplus of £148m in 2022/23 and £1.6bn in 2021/22 — a **£3.2bn swing in two years**.

The deterioration was driven by three structural pressures operating simultaneously:

1. **Pay cost acceleration** — the 2023 Agenda for Change pay award added significant cost to an already pressured workforce budget, pushing sector pay costs to approximately 67% of income
2. **Non-pay inflation** — clinical supplies, drugs, and premises costs continued to rise above the income growth rate
3. **Income constraint** — NHS England's Aligned Payment and Incentive (API) contract uplifts did not fully compensate for cost inflation, leaving a structural gap at most trusts

More than half of all NHS providers — **124 of 206 trusts (60%)** — ended 2023/24 in operating deficit, up from 85 in 2022/23 and 37 in 2021/22.

## 1. Income & Expenditure — Sector Outturn

### 1.1 Three-Year Trend

| | 2021/22 | 2022/23 | 2023/24 | Change 21/22–23/24 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total income | £110.3bn | £118.4bn | £129.2bn | +17.1% |
| Total expenditure | £108.7bn | £118.2bn | £130.8bn | +20.3% |
| Operating surplus / (deficit) | +£1,600m | +£148m | **−£1,620m** | −£3,220m |
| Net surplus / (deficit) | +£960m | −£210m | **−£2,100m** | −£3,060m |
| Trusts in deficit | 37 of 211 | 85 of 207 | **124 of 206** | +87 trusts |

Over the three-year period, income grew by £18.9bn (+17.1%) while expenditure grew by £22.1bn (+20.3%). The expenditure growth rate outpaced income by 3.2 percentage points, eroding the sector's financial position in every year.

### 1.2 Income Composition

Patient care income, which accounts for approximately 83% of total income, is primarily received through NHS England's **Aligned Payment and Incentive (API)** framework. Under API, a fixed element (formerly block contract) provides income certainty while a variable element links payment to activity delivery.

The remaining 17% of income comprises:

| Source | Approx. % of total income |
|--------|--------------------------|
| R&D and education/training | 4–5% |
| Private patient income | 2–3% |
| Other non-NHS clinical income | 2–3% |
| Other operating income | 7–9% |

Trusts with higher proportions of R&D and education income (notably large academic teaching hospitals) showed greater financial resilience, as this income stream is largely uncorrelated with the clinical cost pressures affecting the rest of the portfolio.

### 1.3 Expenditure Composition 2023/24

| Category | Amount | % of Total Expenditure |
|----------|--------|------------------------|
| Pay costs | ~£87bn | ~67% |
| Non-pay costs (excl. depreciation) | ~£35bn | ~27% |
| Depreciation and amortisation | ~£8bn | ~6% |

Pay costs are the dominant cost driver and the primary lever available to management. However, the NHS workforce is governed by national pay frameworks (Agenda for Change, Medical and Dental pay review), limiting the ability of individual trusts to reduce pay costs independently.

## 2. Pay Cost Pressure Analysis

### 2.1 Sector Pay Trend

| | 2021/22 | 2022/23 | 2023/24 | YoY change (pp) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sector pay % of income (Acute) | 64.2% | 65.1% | 67.3% | +2.2pp |
| Sector pay % of income (Mental Health) | 68.4% | 69.2% | 71.1% | +1.9pp |
| Sector pay % of income (Community) | 69.7% | 70.3% | 71.8% | +1.5pp |

The 2023 Agenda for Change pay award (effective from April 2023, i.e. from M01 of 2023/24) added approximately £3.0bn to the sector pay bill above the level already funded by NHS England, creating a structural in-year pressure that was only partially offset by central pay award funding.

A trust running at 67% pay-to-income ratio with income growing at 9% but pay costs growing at 11% will experience a structural widening of its deficit regardless of efficiency measures elsewhere.

### 2.2 Pay Pressure by Sector

**NHS benchmark (amber threshold):** Acute ≤65% | Mental Health ≤70% | Community ≤70% | Ambulance ≤68% | Specialist ≤60%

In 2023/24, the majority of acute trusts exceeded the amber threshold on pay as a percentage of income. Ambulance trusts, which carry a high proportion of urgent-response staff, showed the most acute pay pressure relative to their income base.

### 2.3 Implications for Financial Planning

The pay cost pressure has a compound effect: trusts in deficit face NHSE/I financial oversight requirements that constrain their ability to invest in productivity-improving capital. This creates a feedback loop where financially stressed trusts are less able to invest in the transformation needed to reduce their structural costs.

## 3. EBITDA Margin Analysis

EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation and Amortisation) is the primary measure of operational financial sustainability used in NHS provider finance. It measures whether a trust can generate enough cash from its operations to fund its capital programme and service its financial obligations.

### 3.1 EBITDA Margin — Sector Summary 2023/24

| Sector | Providers | Avg EBITDA % | Trusts <0% | Trusts 0–2% | Trusts ≥2% |
|--------|-----------|--------------|------------|-------------|------------|
| Acute | 118 | 3.2% | 28 | 45 | 45 |
| Mental Health | 45 | 4.1% | 6 | 12 | 27 |
| Specialist | 15 | 5.8% | 1 | 3 | 11 |
| Community | 18 | 3.9% | 2 | 6 | 10 |
| Ambulance | 10 | 2.1% | 3 | 4 | 3 |

*Indicative figures derived from TAC data; minor rounding applied.*

### 3.2 Distribution Narrative

The acute sector, which represents the largest share of NHS activity and expenditure, showed the greatest spread in EBITDA margin, ranging from deeply negative (some trusts at −5% or worse) to positive outliers exceeding 10%. This dispersion reflects differences in:

- **Income mix** — trusts with higher private patient and R&D income show structurally higher margins
- **Geography** — London trusts face higher pay costs (outer London weighting) but also benefit from higher-value specialist activity
- **Legacy cost base** — PFI (Private Finance Initiative) hospital trusts carry higher financing costs that reduce operating surplus without appearing in EBITDA; however, PFI service charges (which are non-pay costs) do reduce EBITDA indirectly
- **Scale** — larger trusts benefit from some economies of scale in back-office functions, though the clinical cost base is largely volume-driven

### 3.3 Year-on-Year Deterioration

The average EBITDA margin fell from 4.6% in 2021/22 to 3.6% in 2023/24, a decline of 1.0 percentage point over two years. At the sector level, this represents an erosion of approximately £1.3bn of EBITDA — the difference between a sector capable of funding its capital requirements from operating cash and one that cannot.

## 4. Workforce and Cost per WTE

### 4.1 WTE Overview

| | 2021/22 | 2022/23 | 2023/24 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total sector WTE | ~1.18m | ~1.22m | ~1.26m |
| Total pay costs | ~£78bn | ~£83bn | ~£87bn |
| Avg cost per WTE (£000s) | ~£66k | ~£68k | ~£69k |

*WTE figures are derived from TAC09 (STA0410 — total average WTE); the sector total WTE from this source differs from NHS Digital Workforce Statistics due to definitional differences.*

### 4.2 Cost per WTE by Sector

Cost per WTE varies significantly across sectors, driven by staff mix:

- **Medical-heavy trusts** (e.g. specialist centres, major teaching hospitals) carry higher average costs due to consultant pay scales
- **Community and mental health trusts** typically have lower medical-to-non-medical ratios and therefore lower average cost per WTE
- **Ambulance trusts** have relatively low medical staff ratios but high overtime and on-call costs

The year-on-year increase in cost per WTE (+1.5% in 2023/24) was below the increase in total pay costs (+5.1%), indicating that the pay cost pressure was driven partly by workforce growth as well as the pay award.

### 4.3 Sickness Absence

Sickness absence remains elevated across the sector following the Covid-19 pandemic period. Trusts with higher sickness rates face a double financial impact: direct cost of sick pay and the indirect cost of backfilling absent staff with bank and agency labour, which carries a premium above substantive pay rates and does not attract pension contributions from the employer's perspective.

## 5. Regional Variation

NHS England's seven regional offices show materially different financial profiles:

| Region | Approx. Deficit % | Key Drivers |
|--------|------------------|-------------|
| London | Mixed — range is wide | High pay (London weighting) offset by specialist activity income |
| Midlands | Above average deficit % | Significant acute estate; limited specialist income |
| North East & Yorkshire | High deficit % | Large acute trusts with structural deficits; legacy cost base |
| North West | Mixed | Mix of high-performing FTs and challenged trusts |
| South East | Below average deficit % | Several high-performing Foundation Trusts |
| South West | Mixed | Geographic challenges; recruitment and retention pressures |
| East of England | Mixed | Significant variation between large teaching hospitals and smaller trusts |

London presents a particular analytical challenge: the outer London pay weighting adds cost without a proportionate income uplift under API contracts, structurally disadvantaging medium-sized London trusts compared to their non-London peers.

## 6. Key Risk Factors for 2024/25

Based on the trends identified in this analysis, the following risks are likely to influence the 2024/25 financial position:

### 6.1 Pay — High Risk
A further Agenda for Change pay award in 2024/25 is likely to add cost above funded levels. Unless NHS England increases API contract values proportionately, the structural pay gap will widen. The workforce plan (NHS Long Term Workforce Plan, 2023) aims to expand the workforce but does not address the immediate pay-to-income ratio pressure.

### 6.2 Clinical Negligence — Medium–High Risk
NHS Resolution clinical negligence premiums have increased steadily as the provision for historical claims grows. This is a structural non-pay cost that trusts cannot reduce through efficiency programmes. The premium is set by NHS Resolution based on a trust's risk profile.

### 6.3 Capital Constraint — High Risk
The NHS capital settlement for 2024/25 remains constrained. Trusts unable to fund capital from operating cash (i.e. those with low or negative EBITDA) are dependent on central capital allocations. The backlog maintenance liability across the estate — estimated at over £11bn nationally — continues to grow, increasing the risk of major plant failure and consequent disruption costs.

### 6.4 CIP Delivery — High Risk
NHS Trusts typically set Cost Improvement Programme (CIP) targets of 2–5% of expenditure annually. In a high-inflation environment, CIP must not only generate savings but must outpace the growth in underlying costs. Evidence from comparable financial years suggests that sector-wide CIP achievement is typically 70–80% of target in a financially stressed year.

## 7. Analytical Conclusions

Three conclusions emerge from this analysis:

**1. The sector deficit is structural, not cyclical.**
The consistent deterioration across three years, driven by pay cost growth persistently exceeding income growth, indicates this is not a one-year anomaly. Restoring the sector to balance requires either a sustained increase in API contract values above pay inflation, or a structural reduction in the cost base through workforce redesign — both of which are medium-term interventions.

**2. The distribution of financial performance is widening.**
The gap between the best and worst-performing trusts is growing. A small number of large, well-managed Foundation Trusts with diversified income streams continue to generate positive EBITDA. A growing number of trusts — particularly medium-sized acute trusts in challenged geographies — are in persistent and deepening deficit.

**3. Pay is the primary lever, but it is externally constrained.**
With pay representing approximately 67% of total expenditure and set by national frameworks, individual trust CFOs have limited room to manoeuvre on the largest single cost line. The opportunity for improvement lies in agency spend reduction (agency premium vs substantive rates), sickness absence management, rostering efficiency, and workforce skill mix — all of which require operational investment before generating financial returns.
